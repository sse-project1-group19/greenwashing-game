## Introduction
One of the biggest problems humanity faces currently is global warming. 'Greenwashing' is a term to describe a blunt corporate behavior, like oil companies sponsoring wildlife documentaries while lobbying againt climate legislation. In essence, the term refers to companies *claiming* to have sustainability, while in actuality they do not.

There are a few subtypes of greenwashing: Green-crowding, green-hushing, green-shifting and green-rinsing. Out of all of these, green-crowding is probably the worst. Companies migrate into sustainability coalitions and net-zero alliances to diffuse accountability between them. If one company does not deliver, they are safe from blame because all companies made the same commitments over the same long-term timeline. 
Green-hushing is kind of the opposite. Companies with sustainability programs actively under-report their progress to avoid scrutiny or legal liability for making claims that might not hold under regulation. Green-shifting redirects the responsibility to the consumers, by telling them to shower for 5 minutes, live smaller and turn off lights. Their own footprint is, of course, left unaddressed. This is probably the most common for governments and energy companies.
Finally, green-rinsing is about constantly resetting goals. For example, a company sets a target in 2015 to be net-zero by 2025. In 2023-2024 the company quietly retires that goal, removes it from their history and announces a new goal to be net-zero by 2030, restarting the reputation divident cycle.

It seems that most people are unfarmiliar with the concept of greenwashing and are unable to recognize it. This leads to complacency when a company announces a new net-zero target for the 5th time, or someone gets told to recycle plastic and wear an extra sweater in the cold months. The goal of this project is to educate people on greenwashing, how to recognize it and how companies do it, by placing the users in an environment with a satirical framing of the current state of the world.
By placing the player in the role of the corporation, they experience the incentive that produces greenwashing from the inside: short-term reward, the temptation to put off genuine investment and rationalizationt that competitors are doing the same thing. The game is not trying to tell the player what to think, but rather lets them discover the impact of such actions by themselves.
